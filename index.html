Section 1

Background reading: the science behind it

Read this carefully — understanding the why is what separates a good project from a great one. Judges love when you can explain the science yourself, not just describe what happened.

What is capillary action?

Capillary action is the ability of a liquid to flow through narrow spaces without help from — and sometimes against — outside forces like gravity. It's the same reason a paper towel soaks up a spill, a candle wick stays wet with melted wax, and trees can pull water from their roots all the way to their highest leaves.

Paper towels are made of thin plant fibers (mostly cellulose) woven loosely together. Between and inside those fibers are thousands of microscopic gaps and tubes, far too small to see with just your eyes. Water is drawn into these narrow spaces and travels through them — upward and outward — even though gravity is pulling it down the whole time.

The two forces that make it happen

Capillary action happens because of a tug-of-war between two kinds of molecular attraction:

Adhesion — water molecules are attracted to the cellulose fibers of the paper towel. They stick to the paper's surface, the same way water clings to the side of a glass.

Cohesion — water molecules are also attracted to each other. Each water molecule is polar (it has a slightly positive end and a slightly negative end), so molecules link up through hydrogen bonds, almost like they're holding hands.

When adhesion pulls water onto the fibers, cohesion drags the rest of the water along behind it. Together, these two forces are strong enough to pull water upward through the narrow gaps in the paper, overcoming gravity — at least until the weight of the water climbing the towel finally balances out the pull of capillary action.

Why does the food coloring move too?

Food coloring is dissolved in the water, so its molecules get carried along for the ride. As the water travels up and across the fibers, it drags the dye with it — which is what lets you actually see the invisible process of capillary action happening in real time.

Where you'll see this in real life

  • Plants and trees use capillary action (with a process called transpiration) to move water from roots to leaves.
  • A candle wick draws up melted wax to keep the flame burning.
  • A sponge soaking up a spilled liquid.
  • Fountain pens and felt-tip markers draw ink to the tip.
Key vocabulary to use in your write-up
Capillary action — liquid moving through narrow spaces against gravity
Adhesion — attraction between two different substances
Cohesion — attraction between molecules of the same substance
Cellulose — the plant fiber paper towels are made from
Polar molecule — a molecule with a positive end and negative end, like water

Section 3

Run the experiment

Follow each step in order. Take the photos when prompted — they're what fill your display board later.

① Prepare your cups

  1. Set out 3 clear cups in a row, evenly spaced, about 2–3 inches apart.
  2. Fill the outer two cups about ⅔ full with water. Leave the middle cup empty.
  3. Add 8–10 drops of food coloring to each filled cup — red in one, blue in the other — and stir gently.
📷
Photo 1 Your 3 cups lined up before adding the paper towels, clearly labeled.

② Cut and fold your paper towel strips

  1. Cut paper towels into strips ~2 inches wide and 8–10 inches long.
  2. Fold each strip loosely into thirds lengthwise, like a paper fan.
  3. You need 2 strips: one linking cup 1 → cup 2 (empty middle), one linking cup 2 → cup 3.

③ Place the strips & start the timer

  1. Place one end of a strip in the red cup, the other end in the empty middle cup.
  2. Place one end of the second strip in the empty middle cup, the other end in the blue cup.
  3. Make sure the strips dip into the water but the cups aren't touching.
  4. The instant everything is in place — start your timer below!
